BdaCo must establish a physical head office on the island and appoint a senior representative to report significant events to the Bermuda Monetary Authority (“BMA”). Presently, the BMA offers three types of digital asset business licenses:

  • Class F (Full) License;
  • Class M (Modified) License; and
  • Class T (Test) License.

Businesses operating under a digital asset business license are exempt from capital requirements. However, license holders must submit a minimum net assets evaluation during the application process, reflecting risks such as operational, credit, and market risks, with residual risks subject to net assets or other safeguards.

According to the Digital Asset Business Act 2018, minimum net assets should not fall below:

  • $100,000 for Class M and F licensees
  • $10,000 for Class T licensees

The BMA reserves the right to request additional capital based on the nature, scale, and complexity of the licensee’s operations.

Initial Coin Offerings

The enactment of the Companies and Limited Liability Company (Initial Coin Offering) Amendment Act 2018 (ICO Act) on July 9, 2018, ushered in a regulatory framework for Initial Coin Offerings (ICOs) conducted in or from Bermuda. According to the ICO Act, no entity is allowed to conduct an ICO within Bermuda unless registered as a company with the Registrar of Companies. Furthermore, ICOs are classified as a “restricted activity,” necessitating Bermuda companies to seek consent from the Minister before launching one within Bermuda.

The ICO Act adopts the same definition of digital assets as outlined in the Digital Asset Business Act mentioned earlier. The Fintech Advisory Committee evaluates each application and forwards recommendations to the Minister regarding approval. Applicants are required to furnish comprehensive details regarding product or service development, anticipated completion timelines, as well as information concerning rights, functionalities, features, and transferability.

To ensure compliance with anti-money laundering laws, ICOs must verify and retain the identity of token or coin purchasers. Upon Ministerial approval, the ICO is mandated to publish an “offer document” and submit it to the Registrar of Companies. This document must encompass particulars such as the business or proposed business, project details, rights and restrictions related to digital assets, alongside cautionary language concerning investment risks associated with ICOs. This warning must be prominently displayed in both the offer document and on the ICO platform during the offering period or suspension, detailing:

(a) Known or reasonably foreseeable substantial risks to the project.

(b) Options available to individuals if the subject project of the ICO does not proceed.

(c) Description of any rights associated with the digital assets offered.

(d) Disclaimers regarding guarantees or warranties concerning the project’s development or any other assets associated with the ICO.

The ICO Act delineates two distinct regulatory regimes based on the type of assets issued. ICOs primarily conducted for crowdfunding purposes are governed by the Companies Act 1981, the Limited Liability Company Act, and the Register of Companies. ICOs involving virtual currencies or digital assets fall under the purview of the Digital Asset Business Act 2018 and are supervised by the Bermuda Monetary Authority.

Anti-Money Laundering Regulations

The Digital Asset Business Act has expanded Bermuda’s anti-money laundering (AML) laws to encompass businesses licensed to engage in digital asset activities within the framework of regulated financial institutions under the Proceeds of Crime Act 1997 (POCA Act), the Anti-Terrorism (Financial and Other Measures) Act 2004, and the Proceeds of Crime (Anti-Money Laundering and Anti-Terrorist Financing) Regulations 2008. Consequently, these laws’ obligations are applicable to entities licensed under the Digital Asset Business Act. AML procedures and policies must be integrated into the license application process under the Digital Asset Business Act, and any foreign branches or subsidiaries must also adhere to Bermuda’s AML laws. Furthermore, the Digital Asset Business Act mandates that licensed entities operate prudently, including compliance with Bermuda’s AML legislation.

Senior management of digital asset businesses is required to:

  • Ensure adherence to the Acts and Regulations.
  • Identify, assess, and mitigate money laundering/terrorist financing risks within the organization’s customers, products, services, transactions, delivery channels, outsourcing arrangements, and geographic connections.
  • Conduct AML and sanctions risk assessments, updating findings regularly.
  • Appoint a Compliance Officer at the senior management level to oversee the establishment, maintenance, and effectiveness of AML/ATF policies, procedures, and controls.
  • Appoint a Reporting Officer to handle client disclosures.
  • Screen employees rigorously.
  • Ensure sufficient resources are allocated and periodically trained on AML/ATF policies, procedures, and controls.
  • Regularly audit and test AML/ATF policies, procedures, and controls, addressing any identified issues promptly and adequately.
  • Acknowledge potential personal liability for non-compliance with legal obligations.

Failure to comply with Bermuda’s AML laws can lead to significant penalties. Non-compliance with POCA Regulations constitutes a criminal offense, punishable by up to two years imprisonment and/or a fine of up to US$750,000. The Bermuda Monetary Authority may levy financial penalties of up to US$10 million for each instance of non-compliance with regulations, in addition to imposing restrictions on issued licenses and other disciplinary actions.
