  • providing virtual asset services,
  • offering virtual asset custody services,
  • conducting a virtual asset exchange.

To obtain a cryptocurrency license in the British Virgin Islands, businesses must fulfill specific criteria established by the BVI Financial Services Commission (FSC). These requirements are formulated to ensure that businesses in the crypto industry comply with local laws and BVI cryptocurrency regulations, conducting their operations in a responsible and sustainable manner. The primary conditions for obtaining a license for crypto exchange in the BVI include:

Incorporation: Businesses are mandated to be incorporated in the BVI and have a physical presence within the jurisdiction, which may involve maintaining an office or appointing a registered agent in the BVI.

Fit and Proper Test: All directors, officers, and shareholders of the business must successfully undergo a fit and proper test, evaluating their suitability for holding positions of responsibility within the business.

Business Plan: Businesses are required to submit a comprehensive business plan detailing proposed actions, products or services, target market, and growth strategy.

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF) Compliance: Businesses must demonstrate effective implementation of AML and CTF policies and procedures, with the appointment of a designated compliance officer overseeing compliance with these regulations.

Financial Conditions: Businesses must possess sufficient financial resources to operate in the crypto industry and maintain a minimum level of capitalization specified by the FSC.

Technology Conditions: Businesses need to exhibit adequate technology infrastructure and security measures to safeguard client assets and personal data.

Professional Indemnity Insurance: It is mandatory for businesses to have professional insurance protecting themselves and their clients against losses resulting from negligence, errors, or omissions. Additionally, a company must appoint an auditor.

Risk Assessment: Companies must provide a written risk assessment document outlining the potential risks the VASP may face. This document should specify how these risks are identified, measured, assessed, monitored, controlled, and reported.

Requirements for documentation and information: When applying for a BVI VASP license, the entity is obligated to furnish the following details and supplement the application form with supporting documents as necessary. These details encompass:

  • The physical address of the Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) located in the British Virgin Islands (BVI).
  • Names and addresses of directors and senior executives.
  • Names and addresses of shareholders, along with their shareholding levels.
  • Name and address of the auditor appointed by the VASP.
  • Name and address of the authorized representative.
  • A comprehensive business plan covering various aspects:
  • The applicant’s knowledge, expertise, and experience.
  • Detailed information about the nature, size, scope, and complexity of the VASP.
  • Description of the underlying technology, delivery method of the virtual asset service, and the virtual asset to be utilized.
  • Marketing strategies and expected sources of business for the VASP.
  • Information about the expected human resources capacity of the VASP initially and in the long term.
  • Planned outsourcing arrangements and governance systems to be implemented.
  • Indication of the initial capital, financial projections, and setup costs for the first three years of operation.
  • A written risk assessment specific to the VASP.
  • A regulatory compliance manual.
  • Proposed internal safeguards and data protection systems.
  • Proposed system for handling client assets, custodian relationships, and complaints.
  • Details of the applicant’s legal advisors.
  • Any other information considered appropriate by the Financial Services Commission (FSC).

Entities intending to provide Virtual Assets Custody Services or operate a Virtual Assets Exchange, as defined in the VASP Act, must furnish the Financial Services Commission (FSC) with additional information and assurances. These requirements aim to demonstrate the VASP’s capability to effectively protect client assets and mitigate the heightened risks associated with money laundering and terrorist financing. BVI Crypto-Currency and ICOs:

In recent years, substantial amounts have been raised through initial coin offers (ICOs) for cryptocurrencies, tokens, and other blockchain-based assets. ICO:

ICOs represent an alternative method for securing external funding. Unlike a traditional IPO, where investors acquire a security linked to the success of the issuer or its business group, an ICO involves investors exchanging cash for a new cryptocurrency within a blockchain network.

This digital asset usually manifests as a credit or token, serving as a medium for making purchases within the platform or business funded by the ICO. Instead of utilizing these “utility tokens” for transactions within the application, most investors typically hold the tokens with the anticipation that the success of the underlying enterprise, where the new utility token serves as the primary unit of exchange, will lead to an increase in the utility token’s relative cash value. This is driven by growing demand for the tokens within the application and their potential acceptance as a broader means of exchange.

Regulators generally do not classify most utility tokens as “investments” or “securities.” Consequently, these tokens are not subject to many regulations and restrictions that typically apply to public debt or equity issued by a company. This exemption occurs because the value of a token is determined by the demand for the token itself, rather than returns or repayments from an underlying business operated by the issuer. ICOs thus offer a swift and cost-effective means for startup businesses, early-stage companies, or technology entrepreneurs to access substantial funds, especially when conventional access to capital markets may be limited.

BVI Crypto Exchanges:

SIBA governs the operation of investment exchanges, but its reach is limited to items considered “investments” under SIBA. Therefore, operating an exchange for tokens deemed utility tokens (and exempt from the discussed legislation’s regulatory scope) is not viewed as a regulated activity, as long as nothing classified as an “investment” is used as a medium of exchange for such utility tokens.

Consequently, exchanging utility tokens for other utility tokens, fiat money, or stable coins is not classified as a regulated activity. However, trading utility tokens for a token resembling an investment might likely be subject to regulation.

British Virgin Islands Financial Services Regulatory Sandbox:

The BVI recently introduced the Financial Services (Regulatory Sandbox) Rules, 2020, to address situations where a token is likely to be regulated (under SIBA, FMSA, or other laws) or where an exchange would involve the trading of “investments” (the Regulations).

These Rules establish a “regulatory sandbox” where innovative fintech businesses can operate, provided they adhere to a clearly defined business strategy and serve a limited (pre-determined) number of clients. This allows for the “sandboxing” of an activity, even if a token is considered to fall under financial regulation, postponing full compliance with BVI financial services regulation during the evaluation of the technology.

Cryptocurrency British Virgin Islands Regulated Activities:

In general, if a virtual asset or a product related to virtual assets is deemed an “investment” under the Securities and Investment Business Act (SIBA), a BVI business may be required to obtain a license to engage in certain activities related to that investment.

Under SIBA, the term “investments” encompasses:

  1. Shares, etc.: This includes shares, interests in a partnership, or fund interests.
  2. Debentures, etc.: Encompassing debentures, debenture stock, loan stock, bonds, certificates of deposit, and any instruments acknowledging indebtedness, excluding those for consideration related to the supply of goods or services. It also excludes cheques, bills of exchange, bankers drafts, or letters of credit.
  3. Instruments giving entitlement to shares, debentures: This category involves warrants or other instruments granting the holder the right to subscribe for investments.
  4. Certificates representing investments: Certificates conferring contractual or property rights held by a person other than the person named on the certificate. The transfer of these certificates may occur without the consent of the rights holder.
  5. Options: Covering options to acquire or dispose of an investment, including currency, palladium, platinum, gold, or silver.
  6. Futures: Referring to futures contracts, excluding those made for commercial, non-investment purposes. A contract is considered for investment purposes if it is traded on an investment exchange or expressed to be traded on such an exchange, or on the same terms as an equivalent contract on such an exchange but for commercial, non-investment purposes.
  7. Contracts for differences: This involves rights under a contract for differences or any other contract intended to secure a profit or avoid a loss based on fluctuations in property or an index, excluding contracts involving the delivery of the related property.
  8. Long-term insurance contracts: Encompassing long-term insurance contracts or rights and interests in any investments.
  9. Rights and interest in investments: Covering rights to and interests in any investment.
  10. Specified investments: Anything specified as an investment by relevant regulations.

It is important to note that cash and real estate are not considered investments under SIBA. For example, a virtual asset transaction classified as a “contract for differences” would be regarded as an “investment” under SIBA, as outlined above.

The definition of investment business under SIBA includes the following activities:

  • Dealing in investments
  • Arranging deals in investments
  • Managing investments
  • Providing investment advice
  • Providing custodial services with respect to investments
  • Providing administrative services with respect to investments
  • Operating an investment exchange
