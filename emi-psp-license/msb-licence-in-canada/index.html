MSB licence in Canada

In Canada, businesses providing certain types of financial services are required to obtain a Money Services Business (MSB) licence. This requirement is a key regulatory aspect to ensure transparency in financial transactions and to combat financial crime, including money laundering and terrorist financing. This article provides an overview of the process of obtaining an MSB licence in Canada and discusses the requirements and obligations associated with this licence.

Definition of MSB

In Canada, MSB activities are defined as companies that have at least one of the following operations:

  • Currency exchange
  • Money transfers
  • Issuance or circulation of traveller’s cheques, money orders or bank cheques
  • Selling or buying electronic money

The process of obtaining an MSB licence

  1. Registration with FINTRAC: To become an MSB, a company must register with the Financial Transactions and Reports Authority of Canada (FINTRAC). Registration is required for all companies performing MSB transactions, regardless of the size or volume of their cash transactions.
  2. Preparation of documentation: The company should provide a complete set of documents, including information about the company, details of the owners and managers, and a detailed description of the monetary operations it plans to carry out.
  3. AML/KYC compliance: MSBs must develop and implement anti-money laundering and customer identification (AML/KYC) programmes. These programmes should include customer identification procedures, transaction monitoring, and employee training.

Responsibilities and requirements for MSBs

  1. Record keeping and reporting: Registered MSBs are required to keep detailed records of all transactions and submit regular reports to FINTRAC. This includes reports on suspicious and large cash transactions.
  2. Ongoing monitoring and compliance: Companies should regularly update their AML/KYC programmes in line with changes in legislation and the market environment. This also includes internal audits and staff training.
  3. Sanctions for non-compliance: Failure to comply with MSB licence requirements may result in serious consequences, including fines, suspension or revocation of licence.

Conclusion: The MSB licence plays an important role in the regulation of financial services in Canada. Obtaining and maintaining this licence requires significant effort on the part of businesses, including strict compliance with regulatory requirements. However, it also provides companies with the opportunity to participate in a stable and safe financial environment, contributing to their growth and sustainability in the marketplace.

 Who will be a foreign money services business (FMSB)?

Understanding who qualifies as an FMSB is critical for companies that operate internationally in the financial services industry because they are subject to specific regulatory requirements under Canadian law.

Criteria for determining the FMSB

A company is considered an FMSB if it fulfils the following conditions:

  1. Location outside of Canada: The company must be incorporated and doing business outside of Canada.
  2. Providing money services to Canadian customers: If a company offers and performs money transactions for customers located in Canada, this may automatically result in its classification as an FMSB.
  3. Types of activities covered by the FMSB definition:
  • Transferring money or currency to or from Canada
  • Currency exchange
  • Managing payments or transactions
  • Issuing or managing money orders, travellers cheques or other similar instruments
  • Conducting cryptocurrency transactions, such as cryptocurrency exchanges or transfers

Regulatory requirements for FMSB

Foreign companies classified as FMSBs are subject to FINTRAC (Financial Transactions and Reports Authority of Canada) registration and regulatory requirements, similar to Canadian MSBs. They are required to:

  1. Register with FINTRAC: FMSBs must register with FINTRAC by submitting all required documents and information about their activities before they can operate in the Canadian market.
  2. Develop and implement anti-money laundering (AML) programmes: These programmes should include procedures for customer identification, transaction tracking and reporting, and staff training.
  3. Compliance with Canadian data protection laws: FMSBs must ensure that the processing and storage of Canadian customer data complies with Canadian privacy laws.

Conclusion: Determining and understanding FMSB status is important for any foreign company providing money services in Canada or to Canadian citizens.  Taxation for Money Services Businesses (MSB/FMSB)

Money services businesses in Canada, including both domestic (MSB) and foreign (FMSB) businesses, are subject to strict regulation not only in terms of operational performance, but also with respect to taxation. Effective management of tax liabilities is a key aspect of running this type of business successfully. This article provides an overview of the tax requirements and specifics for MSBs and FMSBs in Canada.

Principal tax liabilities of MSBs and FMSBs

  1. Value Added Tax (GST/HST) All MSBs incorporated in Canada are required to register as GST/HST taxpayers if their annual turnover exceeds CAD 30,000. This tax applies to most services provided by MSBs, including transfer and foreign exchange fees. The GST/HST rate varies depending on the province where the business is carried out.
  2. Income Tax. MSBs, like any other business in Canada, are required to pay income tax. The amount of tax depends on the structure of the business (sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation) and the amount of income. Corporate taxes range from 15% at the federal level to additional taxes at the provincial level.
  3. Personal income tax. For self-employed individuals and members of partnerships working in MSBs, income from MSB activities is subject to income tax as ordinary income. The tax rates are progressive and depend on the total annual income of the individual.

Specifics of FMSB taxation

Foreign MSBs that provide services to Canadian clients but do not have a permanent establishment in Canada are not subject to Canadian income tax. However, if an FMSB has a permanent establishment, such a company will be subject to income tax in the same manner as Canadian companies. It is also important to note that FMSBs must comply with all applicable tax requirements relating to the collection and payment of GST/HST if they sell goods or services that are physically located in Canada.

Who is not an MSB or FMSB in Canada?

Canada, like many other countries, has clearly defined criteria that determine when an entity must register as a money service business (MSB) or a financial intermediary (FMSB). Let's look at who is not considered an MSB or FMSB in Canada and for what reasons.

Categories that do not fall within the definition of MSB or FMSB

  1. traditional banking institutions. Banks and credit unions that are regulated by national banking regulators (e.g., the Canadian Financial Institutions Supervisory Authority) are not considered MSBs. These organisations fall under a different, more stringent regulatory framework.
  2. Professional accountants when performing certain services. Professional accountants who only engage in traditional accounting, auditing or tax services are not considered MSBs. However, if they provide money transfer or currency exchange services, they must register as MSBs.
  3. Legal persons in the standard practice of law. Lawyers and notaries who are only engaged in the provision of legal services are also not MSBs, unless they are involved in conducting financial transactions on behalf of their clients.
  4. Commodity trading companies. Organisations whose main activity is the sale of commodities, including precious metals or precious stones, and which do not offer currency exchange or money transfer services are not classified as MSBs.
  5. Ordinary commercial enterprises engaged in the sale of goods or services. Restaurants, shops and other enterprises that accept payments from their customers in the ordinary course of business are not considered MSBs because their primary function does not involve MSB financial transactions.

Conclusion

It is important to understand that MSB or FMSB status in Canada is awarded based on specific financial activities required by law. What are the AML risks for MSBs in Canada?

Money Service Businesses (MSBs) in Canada face a number of specific AML (Anti-Money Laundering) risks. The specific characteristics of MSBs make them vulnerable to various money laundering schemes, which requires them to carefully comply with regulatory requirements and implement effective internal control systems. In this article we will look at the main AML risks faced by MSBs in Canada and methods to minimise them.

  1. High volume of cash transactions

MSBs often handle large amounts of cash, which makes them attractive to individuals seeking to legalise illegally obtained funds. MSBs should therefore be particularly careful to monitor the origin of cash, vet customers, and track unusual or suspicious transactions.

  1. International translations

Many MSBs provide international money transfer services, which increases the risk of involvement in international money laundering schemes. Companies must ensure compliance with international AML standards, including fulfilment of FATF (Financial Action against Money Laundering) requirements.

  1. Anonymity of transactions

Some MSB activities, especially those related to cryptocurrencies, may provide transaction anonymity. This increases money laundering risks as it makes it difficult to trace the flow of finances. MSBs need to develop mechanisms to identify and verify the identity of customers.

  1. Inadequate internal policies and procedures

The lack of strict internal AML policies and procedures can lead to regulatory violations and the use of MSBs for illegal purposes. It is important that companies have comprehensive AML/CFT compliance systems in place and regularly updated.

  1. Engagement with unregulated or risky sectors

Co-operation or engagement with unregulated sectors or sectors with a high risk of money laundering also increases the risks for MSBs. Partners and service providers should be carefully selected, checking their background and reputation in accordance with AML requirements.

AML risk minimisation methods for MSBs

MSBs should take the following measures to effectively combat money laundering risks:

  • Regularly update and adapt internal policies and procedures;
  • Provide training to staff on AML issues;
  • Utilise advanced technology to monitor transactions and identify customers;
  • Co-operate with law enforcement and regulatory agencies.

Conclusion

Money service businesses in Canada must recognise and actively manage the risks associated with money laundering.
